background

The Berkeley Student Food Collective (BSFC) is a non-profit grocery store entirely run by volunteers. BSFC began in 2008 when a group of students successfully protested against the opening of a Panda Express on the UC Berkeley campus as it was unhealthy, unsustainable, and would ultimately displace local food businesses. In 2009, founding members of this movement sold produce from local farmers markets at cost on Sproul Plaza in order to provide accessible, fresh food options. The BSFC has been dedicated to “providing fresh, local, healthy, environmentally sustainable, and ethically produced food at affordable prices to the Berkeley campus and greater community” in addition to “educating students about nutrition and food systems, empowering new leaders, and training youth to work in and manage a sustainable business.”

define

In an effort to broaden the membership base and be as reflective of the surrounding community as possible, the BSFC asked the Invention Corps:

  • How might we attract and engage new members that are not currently represented in the collective?
  • How can we broaden our membership base?
  • How can we develop a tiered membership system?
  • How can we adjust incentives and obligations based on levels of membership?
  • How can we ensure that this initiative does not undermine our existing membership structure?

Our project mentors and the collective in general recognize how important it is for the food collective to be an accurate representation of the community they aim to serve. Doing so allows them to cater best to those who need the collective’s products and services most acutely. It allows them to make more holistic and productive decisions and shape the future of the collective in the most beneficial way possible.

We kicked off our project process with a meeting with our project mentors in which our team fired questions, confusions, and inquiries at our project mentors, trying to learn more about the collective’s pain points and attempting to face this social issue head on instead of tip-toeing around it. The simple action of coming to a third party to improve faults they recognized within their organization shows the thoughtful humility and deep self-awareness the collective possesses. Our team was moved by our mentors’ hunger to continuously improve the BSFC. We learned the BSFC’s membership skewed towards financial and systemic racial privilege. We learned membership largely grew due to recommendations by friends that were already collective members.

ideate

We began by doing a first round of classic ideation— throwing out all ideas that came to mind, with no idea being too big or too small.

These were some of the major ideas and themes we came up with:

  • Demographically quantify and understand current store patrons to compare to members and note differences
  • Properly understand food insecurity around campus
  • Develop personas for BSFC’s patrons
  • Develop a survey that addresses pain points of the BSFC
  • Survey loyal customers to determine what could entice them to become store members
  • Is the current leadership system effective? How can this be determined
  • How can the current membership incentive structure be changed

After this ideation session, we set out to 1. conduct in depth literature review 2. conduct a survey on current BSFC members to acquire data 3. conduct in person interviews with key organization officials involved with the community and food insecurity. 1 was meant to inform the questions we would develop; 2 would acquire necessary data to maximize current membership; 3 was implemented to derive insights to attract prospective members from the general community.

Literature Review

For our literature review, we found that cooperative success and function, seemed to generally stem from:

  • A sense of community
  • A strict membership work requirement
  • A scaled benefits system
  • A bureaucratic leadership structure
  • Partnerships with other local businesses

Below is our notion table, where we formulated key traits and practices of other food cooperatives as a team

Below are the most important structural differences other food cooperatives have from BSFC that we took specific note of.

Brooklyn Food Co-Op:

  1. Membership required to shop in grocery store, including household rule
    • Your entire household family has to be a member for you to be able to shop at the collective. Moreover, if anyone in the family evades their duties, the entire family loses privileges from the food collective.

Isla Vista:

  1. Point system with rewards, versus a discounts
    • Allows more scalability with commitment, which may draw in prospective members that would seek more freedom
  2. Different membership tiers
    • Higher levels require more commitment, but allow you to have more voting rights and power in the decision making of the collective

ASUW Food Co-op:

  1. More food knowledge and health engagement activities
    • Examples include cooking books, recipe tasting, and cooking events to attract prospective members to the food collective (adds the values of healthy eating and cooking to the collective's pitch, versus it being solely an institution to provide groceries to those in need)

PSFC:

  1. Services not limited to the co-op itself
    • Through external partnerships, PSFC members also get discounts at other local businesses and also provide services like free childcare to children of Coop member.

User Interview of BSFC Membership

Next, we utilized this research to inform and modify polished survey questions, which we planned to administer to current BSFC members.

However, with the unexpected advent of the coronavirus and stay at home policies, our team was suddenly forced to work remotely. Moreover, we received note from our project mentors about how a survey might be difficult to administer due to lower response rates from the now dispersed membership. Instead, we modified our approach, adopting the use of personal user interviews instead of the mass distribution of a general survey. We took this approach because we also believed that an individualized personal interview would evince more detailed and in depth insights, which would not be possible to derive from basic general surveys.

Below are key insights we derived from previous BSFC membership.

It’s a really big group, so it’s hard to get to know people. Adding on to this, meetings are not consistent and there isn’t accountability for attendance. Together, these things make it really hard to really see the food collective as a cohesive community.
  • the large size of the collective and lax attendance restrictions for meetings have made it hard for members to feel connected with the BSFC as a true community
While the board members themselves are very friendly, it is difficult to know who to talk to and how, which makes it feel hard to be heard by the leadership.
  • the leadership may seem friendly and open, but general members might not have easy access to info regarding who to talk to and the overall structural communication process
Different levels of incentive that scale with your commitment would probably increase membership
  • members seem to prefer more flexibility with their membership, where commitment and governing voice in the club scale together
The discount wasn’t really the main reason for my membership, it was more the ideas and principles that the store stood for
  • the ideas and the principles the food collective stands behind are important inherent aspects of the membership itself; the discount is not the sole reason for every member's patronage

User Interview of Key Figures Involved in Food Insecurity & Student Community

For our last step, we conducted personal interviews with organization officials that were directly involved with food collectives and food insecurity.

Below are key insights derived from the Vice President of Student Affairs for Berkeley ASUC

  1. What is your background?
    • Started in student affairs branch her sophomore year
    • Manages outreach to specific communities providing basic needs/self-care and professional assistance on campus
  2. Have you heard of the Berkeley Student Food Collective?
    • A lot of students don’t know about them
    • They offer a lot of cheap healthy options, but students don't know that they are a resource they can utilize
  3. What do you think that the BSFC should do to increase membership?
    • Recommends that BSFC do more promotional work on campus, marketing their location and messaging through flyering or design work.
    • When students want something to eat quickly they often just go to Walgreens, even though Berkeley Student Collective is right there
    • Should conduct pop-up events like the Food Pantry, i.e the Food Pantry sets up a mini-grocery store stand at key areas around campus to raise awareness
    • “Sometimes it's not about students coming to you, but you going to students so they know where you are present.”
  4. How can the BSFC increase its diversity?
    • BSFC is less in a marginalized community (later she clarifies she meant the black community) but is more in the Eco/sustainability community
    • BSFC is more appealing to white and Asian students because they are in the eco-friendly/sustainability spaces and often black students are not as welcome in those spaces (also not that many black students on campus 1-2% of total undergrad student population)
    • “There has to be more intentionality with serving people who are marginalized”
    • The Food Pantry went into the black community center and actively worked to build a bond and trust with those students.
    • “I really like your idea of possibly having barbeques or welcome events where the BSFC are on campus and being present…I think that is a wonderful idea personally.”

Below are key insights derived from the Director of COFED

  1. What is COFED?
    • Started in 2011 in UC Berkeley in response to Panda Express
    • A joint organization that works with multiple food and residential coops, and has a strong emphasize on diversity and inclusion
  2. What is your role in COFED?
    • Director of CoFED, operations
  3. How can BSFC better engage marginalized groups and increase diversity with members?
    • Understand that POC and minorities are marginalized and minoritized. Privilege of others will make marginalized people “insecure” in a volunteer space
    • Really focus on education of the food apartheid
    • Talk about the 10% discount in terms of personal life experience change
    • “Food is a source of pain sometimes.. Where their next meal is coming from”
  4. In your experience, how can food collectives best enhance member experience?
    • Decentralizing education and hosting workshops 
    • Really emphasize bringing types of people you want and need into the collective
    • Connect community with work and making it intergenerational
    • Zoom cooking demo

recap & conclusion

The Berkeley Student Food Collective initially partnered with Invention Corps to address the following questions:

  • How might we attract and engage new members that are not currently represented in the collective?
  • How can we broaden our membership base?
  • How can we develop a tiered membership system?
  • How can we adjust incentives and obligations based on levels of membership?
  • How can we ensure that this initiative does not undermine our existing membership structure?

Initially, our team researched other co-ops around the country to compare their organizational structure and success with the Berkeley Student Food Collective (BSFC). We then used that research to inform detailed survey questions. However, with the sudden advent of the novel coronavirus and new changes, we switched gears to utilize those questions in a personalized interview with former membership rather than administering a general survey.

Moreover, our team conducted interviews with the director COFED and the recently elected vice-president of student affairs in ASUC. These insights enabled us to derive greater understanding of the interconnection between the Berkeley student community and food collectives.

Whether it be greater engagement in marginalized spaces, more emphasis on a tiered/scaled membership system, or increasing community cohesion and attendance in the club, our team has gathered an array of recommendations and insights from detailed literature review and user interviews with both former BSFC members and key figures in the Berkeley community that deal with food insecurity. Throughout this entire document, we have provided a long and comprehensive trove of information and recommended strategies which we believe can enable the Berkeley Food Collective to grow to new heights.
